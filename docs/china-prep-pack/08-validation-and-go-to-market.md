# Validation and Go-to-Market

## Purpose
This document describes how to validate the China Prep Pack concept before overbuilding the product.

The goal is not to launch at scale immediately.
The goal is to prove that:

- users feel the problem strongly enough
- the output is useful enough to keep
- the product feels more valuable than a generic guide
- some users are willing to pay for a full version

---

# 1. Core assumptions to validate

## Assumption 1
First-time travelers to China feel real anxiety around preparation, not just inspiration.

## Assumption 2
A personalized prep pack is more useful than reading multiple posts or asking a generic AI.

## Assumption 3
Users value structure, prioritization, and fallback planning.

## Assumption 4
Some users are willing to pay for a high-quality prep pack.

## Assumption 5
A narrow pre-departure tool can be a strong wedge before broader travel support.

---

# 2. Validation goals for MVP

The first version should answer these questions:

1. Do users complete the onboarding flow?
2. Do users feel the pack is personalized?
3. Do users save or export the pack?
4. Do users say it reduces anxiety?
5. Are users interested enough to pay for a full pack?

---

# 3. What should be tested first

The first thing to test is not growth.
The first thing to test is usefulness.

Priority order:

1. perceived usefulness
2. perceived personalization
3. save or export behavior
4. willingness to pay
5. acquisition channel fit

---

# 4. Validation methods

## Method 1: manual concierge testing
Create packs manually or semi-manually for real users from the target segment.

### Why this is useful
- fastest path to learning
- allows deep feedback
- reveals weak spots in task selection and wording

### Success signal
Users say the pack is clearly more useful than a normal guide.

---

## Method 2: landing page + fake door
Publish a simple landing page that explains the product and invites users to generate a prep pack.

### Test goals
- interest rate
- start rate
- onboarding completion rate
- waitlist or email signup rate

### Success signal
Users are interested enough to begin the flow.

---

## Method 3: prototype output testing
Show users several generated prep packs and ask them to compare.

### Questions to ask
- Does this feel useful?
- Does this feel personalized?
- What is missing?
- Which part is most valuable?
- Would you save this?
- Would you pay for this?

### Success signal
The user can identify one or two parts they would genuinely rely on.

---

## Method 4: lightweight paid test
Offer a free summary and a paid full prep pack.

### Why this matters
Stated interest is weaker than actual purchase intent.

### Success signal
A meaningful percentage of users click through or attempt payment.

---

# 5. Early acquisition channels

The first goal is not scale.
The first goal is to find concentrated user groups.

## Possible channels
- Reddit communities related to China travel
- English-speaking travel communities
- people commenting on China travel content
- solo travel groups
- Muslim travel or special-diet travel groups
- small creator audiences discussing China travel

## Channel principle
Go where users are already asking travel preparation questions.

---

# 6. Suggested first offer

The simplest early offer is:

- free: short summary + top 3 priorities
- paid: full prep pack

A full prep pack can include:
- complete checklist
- arrival-day guide
- communication cards
- personalized risk summary
- printable or exportable version

This is easier to understand than a subscription.

---

# 7. Pricing hypothesis

The first pricing model should be simple and one-time.

Why:
- this is task-based value
- pre-departure prep is naturally one-off
- subscriptions add friction too early

Early pricing can be tested at a modest level to measure seriousness rather than maximize revenue.

---

# 8. Key metrics

## Product metrics
- onboarding start rate
- onboarding completion rate
- pack generation rate
- save rate
- export rate

## Value metrics
- percentage of users who say the pack feels personalized
- percentage of users who say it reduced uncertainty
- percentage of users who say they would use it during real travel

## Commercial metrics
- upgrade click rate
- willingness-to-pay response rate
- payment intent rate

---

# 9. Recommended first experiments

## Experiment 1
Manually produce 5-10 prep packs for realistic personas and review them with real users.

## Experiment 2
Create a simple landing page with one strong value proposition and one CTA.

## Experiment 3
Ship a clickable prototype and ask users to walk through it on mobile.

## Experiment 4
Test a paywall or checkout intent after showing a useful preview.

---

# 10. User feedback questions

After showing a prep pack, ask:

- What part feels most useful?
- What part feels too generic?
- What would make this feel more trustworthy?
- Would you save this before your trip?
- Would this help you feel more prepared?
- Would you pay for the full version?

The goal is to get concrete behavior signals, not vague compliments.

---

# 11. Risks to watch early

## Risk 1: output feels like generic AI text
Mitigation:
- increase structure
- improve rule-based differentiation
- reduce empty summary language

## Risk 2: too many tasks overwhelm users
Mitigation:
- tighten ranking
- show only highest-value tasks first
- simplify optional content

## Risk 3: users like the concept but do not act
Mitigation:
- make output more concrete
- add save/export value
- improve call to action

## Risk 4: users compare it to free AI tools
Mitigation:
- win on structure, relevance, and usability
- emphasize ready-to-use cards and first-day flow

---

# 12. Go-to-market principle

The first go-to-market strategy should be narrow.

Do not try to market to all international travelers.
Start with:
- first-time travelers
- English-speaking users
- high-anxiety use cases
- strong operational pain points

The best wedge is not broad inspiration.
The best wedge is preparation confidence.

---

# 13. Definition of successful early validation

China Prep Pack has early validation if:

- users understand the value quickly
- the product output feels clearly more useful than a guide
- users save or export their pack
- some users attempt to pay for a complete version
- feedback consistently highlights checklist, communication cards, or arrival-day guidance as valuable

If those signals appear, the product deserves deeper productization.
